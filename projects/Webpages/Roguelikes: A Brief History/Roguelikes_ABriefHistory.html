Roguelikes: A Brief History

Written by Evan Cooper

# Introduction

On this page, I will be discussing a specific sub-genre of role-playing games (RPGs) traditionally known as "roguelike".

According to the "Roguelike" entry on Wikipedia ("Roguelike," 2024), a roguelike is a style of RPG traditionally involving the following characteristics:

  • "dungeon-crawl"
    • the primary objective is to explore a dungeon-like setting, fighting monsters and collecting items in pursuit of a final goal

  • procedurally-generated levels
    • the layout and characteristics of game levels are randomized on each playthrough using procedural generation techniques

  • turn-based gameplay
    • events in-game occur on a turn-by-turn basis, typically with level elements updating on each individual keypress by the player

  • grid-based movement
    • player character and NPC movement occurs on a grid structure, with each movement happening across cells of consistent height and width

  • permanent player character death ("permadeath")
    • upon depletion of the player character's health stat, all game progression is lost, requiring players to start from scratch on their next playthrough


# Why Roguelikes?

I have had a fondness for roguelikes ever since I was a kid, when my dad and I would play NetHack together. NetHack was a roguelike that released in 1987 and took inspiration from the original roguelike, Rogue.

As a kid, I had always been a fan of video games, but it was infrequent that my dad and I shared interest in the same game. However, NetHack was a game that my dad had played as a kid, himself, so when he came across a more modern iteration of the game available online and introduced it to me, we instantly bonded over the experience. I have fond memories of him and I spending entire summer afternoons taking turns playing the game. NetHack is notoriously difficult, so we never really "beat" the game, and my dad was, admittedly, much better at it than I was, but I was so excited about finally having a game to share with him, that it never mattered if we won.

Years later, I still have a fondness for roguelikes (though I am still not very good at them). Today, there are much nicer-looking roguelikes being made by indie game developers, complete with more modernized features and functionalities (see Caves of Qud) to meet the standards of today's much more advanced gaming industry.


# A Brief History

What follows is a brief retelling of the history of the roguelike genre -- from the inspiration for the original Rogue, to the most modern renditions of the genre.

Pre-Rogue // Colossal Cave Adventure (1976)

Designed by developer Will Crowther for the PDP-10 mainframe computer and released in 1976, Colossal Cave Adventure is a "text-based adventure game" in which players explore a sprawling, mysterious cave system in search for treasure ("Colossal Cave Adventure," 2023). Players move through and interact with the game world by inputting one- or two-word commands to the game's user interface.

The release of Colossal Cave Adventure proved to be a watershed event in the history of gaming, as it directly inspired the creation of several other games in its era, "including Zork (1977), Adventureland (1978), Mystery House (1980), Rogue (1980), and Adventure (1980), which [became] the foundations of the interactive fiction, adventure, roguelike, and action-adventure genres."

Colossal Cave Adventure (1976)


The Grandfather of Roguelikes // Rogue (1980)

Designed by developers Michael Toy and Glenn Wichman for Unix-based minicomputer systems and released in 1980 ("Rogue [video game]," 2023), Rogue is considered to be the first of its kind, hence why the heavily-expanded-upon genre is still referred to as "Rogue-like" today.

Like its inspiration, Colossal Cave Adventure, Rogue lets players roleplay as an adventurer in an underground labyrinth, searching for treasure -- specifically, the Amulet of Yendor. However, unlike CCA, in Rogue, players are able to control the movement of a character sprite on-screen with specific keypresses, rather than simply imagining the movement of their character through the dungeon, directed by short console commands. Additionally, Rogue is turn-based, with player movement and actions taking place on a square grid, with on-screen elements (such as the player character, non-player characters, enemies, items, and environmental features like walls and doors) represented in ASCII or other fixed character sets in a game world that is entirely procedurally generated on each playthrough. One of the most iconic characteristics of Rogue that has been a staple in the genre since its release is permanent player character death ("permadeath"), which means that a player's playthrough comes to an absolute end whenever their character's health stat is depleted -- a serious challenge not for the faint of heart, which has prevailed into today's newest renditions of the genre.

Rogue (1980)


Early Roguelikes // Hack (1982), Moria (1983)

Though Rogue's developers never released the source code behind their hit title, their game was so popular at the time that it spawned several spin-offs -- the first "Rogue-likes" -- which were created from scratch by their respective developers in order to emulate and expand upon the concepts introduced in Rogue. Of the many early "roguelikes" developed at this time, two of the most influential were Hack and Moria.

Hack was developed originally by Jay Fenlason in 1982, but the most popular iteration of the game (and a much expanded version, at that) was developed by Andries Brouwer and released in 1984 ("Hack [video game]," 2023). In Hack -- much like in Rogue -- game elements are represented on-screen in a graphical user interface using arrangements of ASCII or Extended ASCII character sets. All of the key roguelike characteristics are present in Hack -- the primary objective of the game is to "crawl" (i.e., explore) a labyrinthian dungeon, descending deeper into the earth while fighting monsters and collecting items in pursuit of the Amulet of Yendor (a clear homage to Rogue); levels are procedurally generated and employ a grid-based structure, and the death of a player character means an absolute end to the playthrough (i.e., "permadeath").

Compared to Rogue, Hack introduced improved features and mechanics to the new roguelike genre, including things such as better enemy "AI" (artificial intelligence -- essentially improved code for how enemies interact with their surrounding environments and the player's character), an expanded character creation menu featuring selectable character classes, in-game shops where player characters can purchase items, and new mechanics such as the ability of a player character to eat corpses to gain their powers (Gailloreto, 2020).

Hack (1982)


Moria, an abbreviated version of its full title, The Dungeons of Moria, was developed by Robert Alan Koeneke and released in 1983 ("Moria [1983 video game]," 2023). The player quest in Moria takes inspiration from J.R.R. Tolkien's novel The Lord of the Rings, with the ultimate objective of descending to the lowest depths of the dungeon to slay the final boss, the Balrog. Along with all of the traditional characteristics of a roguelike, Moria helped to evolve the genre by expanding the size, shape, and depth of dungeon levels; by introducing a final boss villain (the Balrog), and giving player characters the ability to mine ore, among other features (Gailloreto, 2020).

Moria (1983)


The Next Generation // Nethack (1987), Angband (1990)

As the popularity of early roguelikes like Hack and Moria took off, they, in turn, inspired their own spin-offs, leading to the development of NetHack and Angband, respectively, as fans of the original games were inspired to create their own renditions of the genre, building upon the features and mechanics found in the originals.

NetHack is actually a "fork" of the original source code for Hack ("NetHack," 2023). Mike Stephenson was the maintainer of the Hack source code and was inspired to create a new-and-improved iteration of the game. Stephenson teamed up with Philosophy professor Izchak Miller and computer hacker Janet Walz, and -- aptly naming themselves the "DevTeam" -- the three set to work expanding and improving the Hack source code, naming their "fork" "NetHack" due to the nature of the DevTeam's collaboration on the game occuring over the Internet. The first version of NetHack was released on July 28, 1987. With NetHack, the DevTeam introduced new player character classes, new enemies, new items, and new dangers for player characters to navigate, while maintaining the same player objective from Hack -- to retrieve the Amulet of Yendor -- and the core characteristics of the roguelike genre (Gailloreto, 2020).

NetHack (1987)


Angband, developed by Alex Cutler and Andy Astrand and released in 1990, in a fashion similar to NetHack, is itself a "fork" of the original Moria ("Angband [video game]," 2024). Angband has a similar structure to Moria, but is instead set in the fortress, Angband, rather than the dungeons of Moria, and its primary objective is for player characters to survive its 100 floors in order to defeat the final boss, Morgoth. Angband built upon the original Moria by expanding the size and complexity of the original game, as well as introducing new features, such as a mechanic where player characters could encounter the undead corpse of their previous characters as an enemy in new playthroughs.

Angband (1993)


Improvements to both games have continued to be made over the years, such as the creation of enhanced graphical interfaces, as with Nethack's "tiles mode" for the Microsoft Windows operating system, which added sprites to represent characters and objects in the game, rather than relying on ASCII characters to display the game world.

The Modern Roguelike // Caves of Qud (2015)

In the years since the release of NetHack and Moria, many different games have been developed drawing inspiration from the early days of the roguelike genre, building upon its core characteristics and adding their own unique twists. Some games have tried to stay true to fundamental roguelike concepts such as "permadeath" and procedurally-generated levels, but, in my opinion, only one game has truly managed to strike a balance between honoring the characteristics of traditional roguelikes and introducing unique twists to those traditional concepts and mechanics of the genre -- that game is: Caves of Qud.

Caves of Qud, developed by Freehold Games, released in "early access" (i.e., a playable state released to the general public, but with the clear intention of the developers to continue development of the game -- adding new features, refining graphics, etc.) on July 15, 2015 ("Caves of Qud," 2023). To quote the developers, Caves of Qud is both a science fantasy RPG and a roguelike epic (Freehold Games, n.d.).

Caves of Qud manages to perpetuate the fundamental aspects of the roguelike genre in the sense that the majority of the game world -- from its items and inhabitants, to large portions of its world, to the histories and relationships of its fictional towns and factions -- are randomly generated using advanced procedural generation techniques; its default game mode adds "permadeath" for player characters, all in-game operations are turn-based, with world elements updating on each keypress of the movement inputs; the world map is grid-based, and -- most importantly -- the game is a "dungeon-crawl," albeit a "crawl" that happens across dozens of "mini-dungeons" in the forms of different towns, biomes, caves, and literal dungeons.

Despite staying true to its roguelike roots, Caves of Qud has also managed to successfully incorporate many elements commonly found in other popular game genres -- primarily role-playing games (RPGs) -- by including features such as a rich narrative "main quest" serving as the thread of primary objectives during a playthrough, and an extensive character creation system that includes features such as selecting special traits, races, and character stats -- an element that is not foreign to the roguelike genre, but which Caves of Qud implements in a form that falls much more in line with traditional RPGs.

With its flawless fusion of both core roguelike elements and concepts and features from other genres such as RPGs, Caves of Qud has managed to become what I would confidently call the "epitomic roguelike", embodying the spirit of its roguelike predecessors, while bringing the core elements up to modern standards and offering a fresh take on the genre.

Caves of Qud (2015)


# Concluding Thoughts

While the games that are listed here are not an exhaustive list of the entire roguelike genre, I believe that they serve as the core thread in its history. From the early days of Rogue, to the modern marvel that is Caves of Qud, the inspiration that has driven developers in the genre for decades -- a love for games, a love of computers, and a love for adventure -- has prevailed, inspiring a new generation of developers to bring fresh takes to the genre and push the boundaries of its defining elements, which likely explains Why the Roguelike Genre Has Become So Popular once again.

To showcase just how popular and varied the roguelike genre has become in today's gaming industry, below is a ranked list of the eight (subjectively) best roguelike games available as of early December 2023 (Sprinks, 2023):

  1. Caves of Qud
  2. Tales of Maj'Eyal
  3. Dungeons of Dredmor
  4. Darkest Dungeon
  5. Tinyfolks
  6. Inkbound
  7. For the King
  8. Crown Trick
